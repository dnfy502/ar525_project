Abstract
Robotic throwing offers a path to fast, long-range
manipulation in industrial settings, but learning reliable release policies demands data efficiency: physical robots tolerate only tens of trials before wear
or safety constraints intervene. MC-PILOT Turcato
et al. [2025] addresses this via a Gaussian-process dynamics model and a single-shot RBF speed policy optimised with Monte Carlo policy gradients, achieving
near-perfect target coverage in roughly ten throws on
a Franka Panda arm.
We reproduce MC-PILOT in an independent ballistic simulator and identify four non-obvious failure
modes that arise when the algorithm’s defaults are
applied outside the paper’s specific geometry. From
this diagnostic process we derive two portable design rules: a lengthscale-to-range scaling rule (ℓs ≈
0.15 × target_range) that prevents RBF blindness in
narrow target domains, and a stratified exploration
policy that provides seed-robust initialisation without additional hardware trials. We further prove a
zero-mean-noise unlearnability result—when noise is
zero-mean the GP residual collapses to a degenerate prior, rendering model improvement impossible—
and demonstrate its practical consequence: a noiseaware policy achieves a 50% hit rate versus 10% for
a naive baseline under 20% velocity slip. Applying
the corrected algorithm across five release heights
(z ∈ {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0} m), we obtain 100% hit
rates on four configurations and a mean landing error
below 35 mm on all five, substantially improving over
the untuned baseline. Finally, we present a complete
wind-aware extension of the pipeline: the state is augmented from 8-D to 11-D to include wind velocity, the
drag equation is corrected to use air-relative speed,
and the policy, GP model, and replay toolchain are
updated consistently. [DATA NEEDED: Quantitative wind results pending.]
1 Introduction
Robotic throwing extends the effective reach of a manipulator far beyond its kinematic workspace, enabling rapid sorting, bin-to-bin transfer, and longrange placement without additional actuators. Industrial pick-and-throw cells can double throughput
compared with pick-and-place because the robot releases the object at high speed and immediately returns for the next grasp while the projectile is still in
flight Turcato et al. [2025]. Despite this appeal, practical deployment is bottlenecked by data efficiency:
learning a reliable release policy on physical hardware must converge in tens of trials rather than thousands, because each trial incurs wear, reconfiguration
overhead, and safety risk. Standard model-free RL
methods require orders of magnitude more samples
than this budget permits, motivating model-based
approaches that explicitly represent uncertainty and
plan through it.
MC-PILOT Turcato et al. [2025] addresses this
challenge by combining MC-PILCO Mitsubishi Electric Research Laboratories [2022]—a Gaussianprocess (GP) dynamics model with Monte Carlo policy gradients—with two throwing-specific modifications. First, the policy is reduced to a single-shot
speed map πθ(P) that fires exactly once at t=0
given the target position P, eliminating the need
for closed-loop feedback during flight. Second, gripper release delay is estimated by Bayesian Optimisation, compensating for systematic timing errors that
would otherwise corrupt every trajectory. The augmented state x˜ = [x, P] lets the GP learn geometryconditioned corrections, and the RBF policy network enables smooth interpolation across the tar1
get workspace. On a Franka Panda arm with a
Gazebo/ROS simulator, MC-PILOT reaches near100% target coverage in approximately ten physical throws—a compelling demonstration of GP-based
model-based RL at the edge of the sample budget.
Despite these results, three practical gaps remain
unexplored. (1) Geometry sensitivity. The published hyperparameters are calibrated to a single release height and target range; we show experimentally
that naïve transfer to different heights silently degrades performance—the RBF policy becomes blind
over most of the target domain because the lengthscale is no longer commensurate with the range, and
random exploration leaves entire sub-regions unvisited. (2) Noise structure. The paper attributes
performance variance to gripper delay, but physical robots also exhibit velocity slip and timing jitter whose statistical structure differs from zero-mean
additive noise; we prove that a zero-mean residual
is unlearnable by the GP and characterise the resulting degeneration analytically. (3) Portability.
Reproducing MC-PILOT requires Gazebo and ROS,
limiting accessibility for researchers without that infrastructure; the algorithm’s core physics is ballistic
and admits a lightweight numpy simulation that decouples the RL problem from the middleware stack.
Our approach is to reproduce MC-PILOT in an independent ballistic simulator augmented with a PyBullet visualisation pipeline, then conduct four focused studies: a release-height generalisation study
varying z ∈ {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0} m; a noise-model
study comparing aware and naive policies under
20% velocity slip; a combined PyBullet-plus-elevated
study providing a visualisable end-to-end demo; and
a wind-robustness study that extends the full pipeline
— simulator, GP, policy, and replay tools — to operate consistently under ambient airflow. Each study
is preceded by a diagnostic phase in which we instrument the algorithm to expose failure modes, formulate a fix, and verify recovery.

2.4 Omitted Components
We intentionally exclude two components from our
reproduction. First, the gripper delay estimation
module, which in the original paper uses Bayesian
Optimisation to calibrate a timing offset between the
commanded and actual release instant Turcato et al.
[2025]: we instead model the effect of release timing
uncertainty directly as a stochastic perturbation in
our noise study (Section 6), making the BO calibration step unnecessary. Second, all hardware-specific
components (ROS communication, Gazebo physics
server, Franka Panda driver) are replaced by a selfcontained numpy ballistic integrator described in Section 3. These omissions are intentional and do not
affect the core learning algorithm under evaluation.
3 System Overview
3.1 Design Rationale: Decoupled Algorithm and Simulation Layers
The implementation is organised around a strict twolayer separation. The algorithm layer contains all
learning components: a Gaussian-process dynamics
model, Monte Carlo policy gradient optimiser, radialbasis-function (RBF) speed policy, and a ballistic
simulator implemented in NumPy and PyTorch. The
simulation/visualisation layer is either (a) the same
NumPy ballistic simulator used for training or (b) a
PyBullet scene with a KUKA iiwa7 arm for validation
and demonstration. These two layers share a single
interface: the commanded release velocity vcmd ∈ R
3
.
3
The central design decision is that the arm’s motion is cosmetic. Ball velocity at release is set programmatically via PyBullet’s resetBaseVelocity to
exactly vcmd, or vcmd+ϵ when sensor noise is enabled.
Arm joint trajectories are planned and executed for
visual fidelity, but any joint-tracking error has no effect on the ball’s actual initial velocity. Consequently,
GP training data are never corrupted by kinematic
imprecision, and the algorithm’s data-efficiency guarantees from Turcato et al. [2025] transfer intact to the
visualisation environment.

3.4 PyBullet Arm Pipeline
The ArmController class wraps a KUKA iiwa7
URDF (loaded from pybullet_data) mounted at a
configurable base position. The throw execution proceeds in five steps:
1. IK for release pose. The desired release Cartesian position is converted to joint angles via PyBullet’s built-in IK solver.
2. Joint velocity mapping. A Jacobian pseudoinverse maps the desired end-effector velocity
to joint velocities, subject to scaled iiwa7 hardware limits (vel_limit_multiplier parameter).
3. Three-phase cubic trajectory. A piecewise
cubic spline drives the arm through neutral →
windup → release → follow-through waypoints.
4. Ball attachment and release. A
JOINT_FIXED constraint (attach_ball())
rigidly couples the ball to the end-effector; the
constraint is removed at the release timestep
(release_ball()).
5. Velocity override. Immediately after constraint removal, resetBaseVelocity sets the
ball’s linear velocity to vcmd. Post-release drag
is applied manually each simulation step via
applyExternalForce using Eq. 35.
The vel_limit_multiplier parameter only governs whether plan_throw() raises a feasibility warning; it does not alter the ball’s actual trajectory,
which is governed entirely by vcmd.

4 Implementation and Diagnosis
Reproducing MC-PILOT Turcato et al. [2025] from
the MC-PILCO codebase Mitsubishi Electric Re4
search Laboratories [2022] revealed four failure modes
that are individually sufficient to prevent any learning from scratch. None of these defects cause an obvious runtime crash in the general case; each manifests
instead as degenerate-but-silent optimiser behaviour.
We document each below using the structure: Symptom → Root cause → Fix → Evidence.
4.1 Broken Policy Gradient Path
Symptom. During early training runs the optimiser
raised RuntimeError: element 0 of tensors
does not require grad and does not have a
grad_fn on the very first backward pass. Even after
patching the immediate exception, cost values were
constant across optimisation steps, confirming that
no gradient reached the policy parameters.
Root cause. The MC-PILCO codebase separates particle propagation into two stages:
apply_policy() computes the commanded
speed u from the RBF policy and passes it to
the simulator, and data_to_gp_input() preprocesses the augmented state before GP inference.
data_to_gp_input() strips all input dimensions
that do not appear in the GP’s training index
set — including the policy-derived speed channel.
As a result, the computation graph from policy
weights θ through u to the GP output was severed;
cost.backward() found no path to θ.
Fix. The commanded speed u is embedded directly
into the particle initial state rather than being passed
as a separate GP input. Given target bearing ϕ =
atan2(Py − y0, Px − x0) and fixed elevation α = 35◦
,
the 3D release velocity is:
vx = u cos α cos ϕ, (7)
vy = u cos α sin ϕ, (8)
vz = u sin α. (9)
All three operations (atan2, cos, sin) have welldefined PyTorch autograd implementations. The velocity vector v = [vx, vy, vz]
⊤ is written into particle state dimensions 3:6 before the rollout begins, so
every GP call and cost evaluation has an unbroken
computation graph back to θ.
Evidence. After this fix, cost.grad_fn is nonNone at every optimisation step, and policy parameters update from the first backward pass onward.
The cost trajectory exhibits monotone descent from
step 1, confirming gradient flow.
4.2 Post-Landing GP Hallucination
Symptom. Before any fix, the per-trial cost
plateaued at ≈ 0.998 regardless of target distance
or policy speed, and optimisation produced no improvement over the Nexp = 5 exploration throws. Inspecting particle states revealed that some particles
had z ≪ 0 (up to −3 m) at the terminal timestep
T = 1.0 s.
Root cause. The simulation horizon in the paper is T = 1.0 s with Ts = 0.01 s (= 100 timesteps).

3.5 m/s lands in approximately 0.58 s under realistic
drag. The MC-PILCO codebase continued propagating all particles through the remaining ≈ 42 timesteps
after landing, feeding underground positions (z < 0,
vz still negative or zero) as GP inputs. These states
lie far outside the GP’s training support, so the posterior mean is governed by the zero prior and the
posterior variance is maximal. The resulting pseudodynamics drove particles to arbitrary positions, and

4.4 Simulation Horizon and Indexing
Bugs
Two further bugs related to simulation horizon and
data-structure indexing caused silent logical errors
with the original paper parameters.
Symptom A: T_control mis-specification.
The MC-PILCO interface expects a number-of-steps
integer for the control horizon. The paper reports
T = 1.0 s. Passing T = 1.0 directly caused the internal computation control_horizon = int(T / Ts)
= int(1.0 / 0.01) = 100... to be applied to what
the codebase treated as already-an-integer, yielding
an effective horizon of T ·(1/Ts)
2 = 10,000 steps. The
resulting rollout allocated a 10,000 × M particle tensor, exhausting RAM and crashing on any machine
with less than [DATA NEEDED: measured peak
RAM] of available memory.
Fix A. The argument is passed as T_control =
int(T / Ts) = 50 (steps, for T = 1.0 s and Ts =
0.02 s), matching the codebase’s expected type.
Symptom B: opt_steps_list index out
of range. MC-PILCO indexes a pre-allocated
list opt_steps_list by trial_index, where
trial_index starts at Nexp − 1 = 4 and increments.
The list was initialised with Nopt entries (one per
optimisation trial), so trial_index = 4 immediately accessed an out-of-range position, raising
IndexError at the start of the first learning trial.
Fix B. opt_steps_list is allocated with length
Nexp + Ntrials, ensuring valid access for all indices.
Horizon optimisation. Beyond the above correctness fixes, the simulation horizon was reduced
from T = 1.0 s (Ts = 0.01 s, 100 steps) to T = 0.7 s
(Ts = 0.02 s, 35 steps). The maximum ball flight
time at uM = 3.5 m/s is ≈ 0.58 s; no particle can be
airborne beyond 0.7 s. Combined with the landedfreeze mask (Section 4.2), this halves the number of
GP evaluations per rollout and yields an approximately 3× wall-clock speedup with no accuracy regression, verified by comparing landing-error distributions across seeds.
Remark on exploration policy. Algorithm 1
of Turcato et al. [2025] initialises exploration with
an analytically-derived policy (Eq. 13 therein) that
computes near-optimal parabolic throws for each target. Applied in our setting this produced all exploration throws concentrated in a narrow speed band
[3.1, 3.4] m/s, causing the GP to overfit to a small
region of velocity space. Hit rate dropped from 50%
(random exploration) to 20% (paper’s analytical exploration) when both were tested under identical
seeds. We therefore use uniform-random exploration
across [um, uM] = [0.75, 3.5] m/s for all experiments,
and document the analytical policy as a failed experiment.

The failure mode with random exploration.
Config B with random seed=1 achieved 0/10 hits

6 Study 2: Arm-Side Stochasticity
6.1 Motivation
The NumPy ballistic simulator is deterministic: given
a commanded velocity vcmd, the landing position is
a fixed function of that input. A closed-form ballistic inverter could therefore solve the task in one step,
rendering the GP-based learning loop superfluous. In
a physical system, the gap between commanded and
delivered velocity arises from several sources: jointtorque saturation, gripper compliance, and release
timing uncertainty. MC-PILOT Turcato et al. [2025]
explicitly models the last source using a gripper delay parameter estimated by Bayesian optimisation (§5
of the original paper). We replace that calibration
step with an explicit stochastic noise model injected
at the velocity-override interface (Section 3), which
lets us study the effect of arm-side stochasticity in a
controlled, parameter-varied manner while preserving
exact GP-gradient compatibility.

8 Study 4: Wind Robustness
Outdoor and atrium-deployed throwing systems must
operate under non-zero ambient airflow. This study
extends the entire MC-PILOT pipeline — simulator,
GP model, policy, and replay tools — to operate consistently under wind.
8.1 Design Principle
The key principle is that wind must be represented
consistently across every component: if wind changes
the dynamics, it must appear in the simulator, the
learning state, and the policy input simultaneously.
Injecting wind only into the physics while leaving
the GP and policy unaware would cause the learned
model to fit windy trajectories without access to the
disturbance that caused them, leading to degraded
prediction accuracy and a policy that cannot compensate airflow.

9.2 Zero-Mean Noise as GP Regularisation
An unexpected finding emerged from the noise study:
the additive observation noise σ = 0.04 m/s (introduced to model velocity scatter) is not merely a noise
source for the aware/naive comparison—it is a necessary regulariser for GP hyperparameter estimation.
When σ = 0 and exploration produces five noiseless, deterministic ballistic trajectories, maximum
likelihood estimation (MLE) of GP hyperparameters
finds that the mathematically easiest fit is a nearly
flat function with effectively infinite lengthscales (observed values: ℓ ≈ 1791 m, 1602 m, 357 m over a
domain of ∼1 m in position and ∼3 m/s in velocity). A GP with lengthscale ≫ domain width predicts the same landing position regardless of launch
speed, making policy gradients effectively zero. With
σ = 0.04, MLE must find finite lengthscales that explain why throws with similar speeds land at different positions; the resulting structure is what allows
the aware policy to learn the 1/(1−α) compensation
multiplier (Proposition 1 and Appendix B).
As established in Proposition 1 (Section 6.3), symmetric zero-mean noise does not shift the optimal
mean action of the policy. The proof is given in Appendix B.
The practical implication for simulation-only experiments is that practitioners should add a small artificial observation noise σn > 0 to GP training data
even in noiseless simulators, treating it as GP regularisation rather than a physical effect. A value of
σn ≈ 0.04 m/s is sufficient for the throwing domain.
9.3 Decoupled Arm–RL Pipeline
Setting the ball velocity explicitly via
resetBaseVelocity at release decouples arm
tracking quality from GP training quality. Even with
substantial joint-tracking error (the iiwa7 position
controller achieves ≈ 7% EE velocity tracking error
in our experiments), the ball always receives exactly
vcmd, and GP training data are never corrupted by
kinematic imprecision.
This design is not merely a simulation convenience.
It corresponds physically to a gripper that can be programmed to release at a precisely measured EE velocity (read from the joint encoder forward kinematics).
In that case, the delivered velocity equals the measured velocity at the release instant, and the arm controller only needs to bring the EE to the desired pose
and velocity—not to track a continuous trajectory
with sub-millimetre precision. The RL algorithm’s
data efficiency guarantees are preserved regardless of
trajectory-tracking quality, provided the release velocity is accurately measured or commanded.
9.4 Limitations
Simulation only. All experiments are conducted
in simulation. Hardware validation on a physical manipulator is needed to confirm that the learned policies transfer and that the 5–10% PyBullet–NumPy
drag discrepancy does not compound with real-world
effects. The wind-aware pipeline (Study 4) is fully
implemented but quantitative results are pending; in
particular, it remains to measure how hit rate degrades with wind magnitude and whether the windconditioned policy recovers the same 100% hit rate
achieved in the no-wind regime.
Fixed launch angle. The elevation angle α = 35◦
is fixed across all experiments. Joint optimisation
of α and release speed would increase the reachable
target volume and potentially improve data efficiency
by concentrating GP data along the optimal ballistic
arc.
Single-seed statistics. Most results are reported
for seed=1 only; Study 1 Config B was also evaluated
at seed=2 for robustness verification. Conferencelevel statistical claims require ≥ 5 seeds to bound
performance variance. The seed=1 vs seed=2 comparison for Config B (random exploration) illustrates
that hit rate can vary between 0/10 and 10/10 for
an unlucky vs lucky seed, confirming that single-seed
results should be read as existence proofs rather than
expected-case performance.
Particle freeze approximation. Once a particle’s z-coordinate falls below zero, its state is frozen at
the previous timestep rather than interpolated to the
exact z = 0 crossing. This introduces a systematic
over-estimation of the landing distance proportional
to the vertical velocity at the last step above ground.
For the simulation timestep Ts = 0.02 s and typical

10 Conclusion
We presented a diagnostic reproduction of MCPILOT Turcato et al. [2025] in a self-contained
14
NumPy and PyBullet simulator, and conducted three
focused studies to characterise how the algorithm behaves outside its original calibration geometry.
Three technical contributions emerge from this
work. First, we diagnosed and corrected four nonobvious failure modes in the baseline implementation:
a broken gradient path from cost to policy parameters (T_control unit mismatch), cost saturation from
excessive lengthscale ℓc relative to exploration error
magnitude, a wasted simulation horizon due to particles propagating after landing, and an index-range
error in the early stopping infrastructure. Each failure was identified by instrumentation, understood
through the algorithm structure, and resolved with
a minimal targeted fix.
Second, two transferable design rules emerged from
Study 1. The lengthscale-to-range scaling rule—
ℓs ≈ 0.15 × target_range—prevents the RBF policy from becoming blind over narrow target domains,
a failure mode that is invisible from the cost curve
alone. The stratified exploration rule partitions the
speed range into equal bands and samples one throw
per band, providing seed-robust GP coverage in the
same number of exploration throws as random sampling while eliminating the max-speed local minima
that trapped random exploration on elevated configs.
Third, the release-height generalisation
study demonstrates 100% hit rates on
z ∈ {0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0} m with mean landing errors below 35 mm across all four configurations, and
10/10 hits on Config E (z = 0.0 m) after iterative diagnosis through five progressive fixes. These results
confirm that the corrected algorithm generalises
across a 2 m range of release heights without any
per-height tuning beyond the three parameters (ℓM,
T, ℓs) that are geometrically motivated.
Study 4 presents a complete wind-aware extension of the pipeline. The drag model is corrected
to use air-relative velocity vrel = p˙ − w, resolving
a physical inconsistency in the original implementation. The throwing state is expanded from 8-D to 11-
D by appending wind velocity w = (wx, wy, wz), and
this context is propagated consistently through the
GP model input, the RBF policy (which now conditions on both target and wind), the MC rollout, the
training scripts, and the PyBullet GUI replay tool.

D Particle Freeze Approximation at Landing
The GP propagates M particles forward in time. Once a particle’s z-coordinate satisfies z ≤ 0, its state is
frozen at the last step above ground and the cost is evaluated at that frozen landing position for all remaining
timesteps.
This introduces a systematic over-estimation of the horizontal landing distance proportional to the residual
horizontal velocity at the frozen step. For timestep Ts = 0.02 s and a typical vertical velocity of 2 m/s at
impact, the true ground crossing occurs ∆t = zlast/vz ≈ 0.01 s before the frozen step, during which the ball
travels an additional ∆x ≈ vx · ∆t ≈ 2–4 cm horizontally (for vx ≈ 2–4 m/s).
In actual rollouts (NumPy and PyBullet), landing is detected at z ≤ 0 and refined by linear interpolation
to the exact z = 0 crossing, so training data are not affected by this error. The approximation applies
only to the Monte Carlo particles used for the gradient estimate inside apply_policy, where implementing
per-particle interpolation would break batched tensor operations. The resulting cost estimates are slightly
pessimistic (the policy is penalised for landing a few centimetres beyond the target when the true landing is
closer), but the bias is consistent across particles and policy updates, so optimisation still converges to the
correct policy. Reducing Ts or implementing a soft landing indicator (a smooth sigmoid of z rather than a
hard step) would eliminate this error at the cost of increased computation.
