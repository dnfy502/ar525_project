\begin{abstract}
Robotic throwing offers a path to fast, long-range manipulation in industrial settings,
but learning reliable release policies demands data efficiency: physical robots tolerate
only tens of trials before wear or safety constraints intervene.
\mcpilot{}~\cite{turcato2025mcpilot} addresses this via a Gaussian-process dynamics
model and a single-shot RBF speed policy optimised with Monte Carlo policy gradients,
achieving near-perfect target coverage in roughly ten throws on a Franka Panda arm.

We reproduce \mcpilot{} in an independent ballistic simulator and identify four
non-obvious failure modes that arise when the algorithm's defaults are applied outside
the paper's specific geometry.
From this diagnostic process we derive two portable design rules: a
\emph{lengthscale-to-range scaling rule} ($\ell_s \approx 0.15 \times
\text{target\_range}$) that prevents RBF blindness in narrow target domains, and a
\emph{stratified exploration policy} that provides seed-robust initialisation without
additional hardware trials.
We further prove a zero-mean-noise unlearnability result---when noise is zero-mean the
GP residual collapses to a degenerate prior, rendering model improvement impossible---and
demonstrate its practical consequence: a noise-aware policy achieves a 50\% hit rate
versus 10\% for a naive baseline under 20\% velocity slip.
Applying the corrected algorithm across five release heights
($z \in \{0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$\,m), we obtain 100\% hit rates on four
configurations and a mean landing error below 35\,mm on all five, substantially
improving over the untuned baseline.
\end{abstract}
