\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Robotic throwing extends the effective reach of a manipulator far beyond its kinematic
workspace, enabling rapid sorting, bin-to-bin transfer, and long-range placement without
additional actuators.
Industrial pick-and-throw cells can double throughput compared with pick-and-place
because the robot releases the object at high speed and immediately returns for the next
grasp while the projectile is still in flight~\cite{turcato2025mcpilot}.
Despite this appeal, practical deployment is bottlenecked by \emph{data efficiency}:
learning a reliable release policy on physical hardware must converge in tens of trials
rather than thousands, because each trial incurs wear, reconfiguration overhead, and
safety risk.
Standard model-free RL methods require orders of magnitude more samples than this budget
permits, motivating model-based approaches that explicitly represent uncertainty and plan
through it.

\mcpilot{}~\cite{turcato2025mcpilot} addresses this challenge by combining
\mcpilco{}~\cite{mcpilco}---a Gaussian-process (GP) dynamics model with Monte Carlo
policy gradients---with two throwing-specific modifications.
First, the policy is reduced to a single-shot speed map $\pi_\theta(\mathbf{P})$
that fires exactly once at $t{=}0$ given the target position $\mathbf{P}$, eliminating
the need for closed-loop feedback during flight.
Second, gripper release delay is estimated by Bayesian Optimisation, compensating for
systematic timing errors that would otherwise corrupt every trajectory.
The augmented state $\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = [\mathbf{x},\, \mathbf{P}]$ lets the GP learn
geometry-conditioned corrections, and the RBF policy network enables smooth interpolation
across the target workspace.
On a Franka Panda arm with a Gazebo/ROS simulator, \mcpilot{} reaches near-100\% target
coverage in approximately ten physical throws---a compelling demonstration of GP-based
model-based RL at the edge of the sample budget.

Despite these results, three practical gaps remain unexplored.
\textbf{(1) Geometry sensitivity.}
The published hyperparameters are calibrated to a single release height and target range;
we show experimentally that na\"ive transfer to different heights silently degrades
performance---the RBF policy becomes blind over most of the target domain because the
lengthscale is no longer commensurate with the range, and random exploration leaves
entire sub-regions unvisited.
\textbf{(2) Noise structure.}
The paper attributes performance variance to gripper delay, but physical robots also
exhibit velocity slip and timing jitter whose statistical structure differs from
zero-mean additive noise; we prove that a zero-mean residual is unlearnable by the GP
and characterise the resulting degeneration analytically.
\textbf{(3) Portability.}
Reproducing \mcpilot{} requires Gazebo and ROS, limiting accessibility for researchers
without that infrastructure; the algorithm's core physics is ballistic and admits a
lightweight numpy simulation that decouples the RL problem from the middleware stack.

Our approach is to reproduce \mcpilot{} in an independent ballistic simulator augmented
with a PyBullet visualisation pipeline, then conduct three focused studies: a
\emph{release-height generalisation study} varying $z \in \{0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$\,m;
a \emph{noise-model study} comparing aware and naive policies under 20\% velocity slip;
and a combined analysis that links the two.
Each study is preceded by a diagnostic phase in which we instrument the algorithm to
expose failure modes, formulate a fix, and verify recovery.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Independent simulation reproduction.}
        A self-contained ballistic simulator and PyBullet visualiser that replicate
        \mcpilot{} without Gazebo or ROS, with all adaptation decisions documented.

  \item \textbf{Four diagnosed failure modes.}
        Systematic identification and correction of non-obvious failure modes including
        RBF lengthscale mismatch, biased exploration, cost-scale sensitivity, and
        GP prior collapse under zero-mean noise.

  \item \textbf{Lengthscale-to-range scaling rule.}
        The heuristic $\ell_s \approx 0.15 \times \text{target\_range}$ prevents RBF
        blindness and transfers across all five tested heights without per-height tuning.

  \item \textbf{Stratified exploration policy.}
        A deterministic coverage strategy that partitions the target workspace into equal
        angular sectors, achieving seed-robust initialisation with the same number of
        exploration throws as the baseline random strategy.

  \item \textbf{Zero-mean-noise unlearnability result.}
        A formal proposition showing that GP residuals under zero-mean noise converge to
        the prior, making model improvement impossible, with an experimental demonstration
        of 10\% vs.\ 50\% hit rate for naive vs.\ noise-aware policies under 20\%
        velocity slip.

  \item \textbf{Release-height generalisation.}
        100\% hit rates on four of five height configurations and mean landing errors
        below 35\,mm across all five, demonstrating that the corrected algorithm
        generalises across a 2\,m range of release heights.
\end{enumerate}
