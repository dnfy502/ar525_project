\section{Study 1: Release-Height Generalisation}
\label{sec:study1}

This study investigates how \mcpilot{} scales across release heights ranging from
ground level to 2.0\,m.
Raising the release point increases the projectile flight time, shifts the reachable
target zone outward, and changes the speed-to-distance mapping that the GP and policy
must learn jointly.
All experiments use the NumPy ballistic simulator (\texttt{mc-pilot-elevated/}) with
no sensor noise; every variable except the trio $(z_{\mathrm{release}},\, \ell_M,\, T)$
is held fixed at the baseline values from Section~\ref{sec:system}.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Stratified Exploration}
\label{sec:study1:stratified}

\paragraph{The failure mode with random exploration.}
Config B with random seed=1 achieved 0/10 hits despite the algorithm being
structurally identical to Config A.
Post-hoc inspection of the exploration throws reveals the mechanism: seed=1 happened
to draw five low-speed samples whose landing positions were 0.245\,m and 0.408\,m from
the origin — far below the closest target at $\approx 1.43$\,m.
The GP was therefore trained on data covering only the low-speed region of the
dynamics, with no observations near the speed range needed to reach any target.
When the policy gradient optimiser subsequently drove commanded speed toward $u_M$,
the GP predicted landing positions based on an extrapolation it had never been asked
to calibrate.
The policy saturated at $u_M$ and all 10 evaluation throws overshot or missed
every target.

\paragraph{The fix: stratified band sampling.}
We introduce \texttt{Stratified\_Throwing\_Exploration}, which partitions the
commanded-speed interval $[0, u_M]$ into $N_{\mathrm{exp}}$ equal bands and samples
exploration throw $i$ uniformly from band $i$:

\begin{equation}
  v_i \sim \mathcal{U}\!\left(
    \frac{(i-1)\,u_M}{N_{\mathrm{exp}}},\;
    \frac{i\,u_M}{N_{\mathrm{exp}}}
  \right),
  \quad i = 1, \ldots, N_{\mathrm{exp}}.
  \label{eq:stratified}
\end{equation}

This guarantees that the GP receives at least one observation in each fifth of the
speed range, regardless of the random seed.
The scheme adds no computational overhead: each exploration throw is still a single
real interaction.

\paragraph{Effect.}
Applying stratified exploration to Config B with seed=1 recovers 10/10 hits
(\emph{B-Strat} row, Table~\ref{tab:height_configs}), confirming that the failure was
entirely due to unrepresentative initialisation, not a fundamental algorithmic
limitation.
Random seed=2 also achieves 10/10, consistent with the hypothesis that seed=1 was an
unlucky draw from the exploration distribution.

\subsection{Config E: A Progressive Diagnosis of RBF Blindness}
\label{sec:study1:config_e}

Config E ($z_{\mathrm{release}} = 0.0$\,m, target range $[0.75, 1.10]$\,m, range
width $\Delta = 0.35$\,m) represents the corner case where the required speed
range is narrow relative to the policy's RBF lengthscale.
The required speed window is approximately $[2.80, 3.39]$\,m/s — only 17\% of
$[0, u_M] = [0, 3.5]$\,m/s.
Five consecutive diagnosis iterations were needed to isolate and fix the failure.

\paragraph{E-Strat: stratified exploration over $[0, u_M]$ (0/10).}
The baseline stratified scheme with $N_{\mathrm{exp}} = 5$ fails: the cost plateaus
near 0.25 and the policy does not converge.
Inspection reveals that only the highest-speed band (band 5, $\approx [2.8, 3.5]$\,m/s)
produces throws landing in the vicinity of the targets.
Exploration throws from bands 1–4 land well short of the target zone, providing no
useful GP supervision for the relevant speed range.

\paragraph{E-Strat2: targeted exploration over $[2.5, u_M]$ (0/10).}
Restricting stratification to the relevant speed sub-interval $[2.5, u_M]$ ensures
all five exploration throws land near the targets, yet the cost plateau persists.
This rules out data coverage as the bottleneck and implicates the policy architecture.

\paragraph{E-Strat3: targeted exploration, $N_{\mathrm{exp}} = 10$ (0/10).}
Doubling exploration throws to 10 while keeping targeted stratification leaves the
plateau unchanged.
Post-optimisation inspection reveals that the policy outputs \emph{exactly} $u_M =
3.5$\,m/s for every target position in the workspace — the $\tanh$ saturates.
With the default lengthscale $\ell_s = 1.0$\,m, all RBF basis functions evaluate
to nearly the same value at every point within the 0.35\,m-wide target zone, so the
policy gradient is negligibly small everywhere; the optimiser is effectively gradient-blind.

\paragraph{E-Strat4: reduced cost lengthscale $\ell_c = 0.25$ (0/10).}
Reducing the cost acceptance width to sharpen gradient signals does not help: the ball
lands at exactly 1.156\,m for \emph{every} trial, confirming that the policy is
outputting a constant speed regardless of target.
The failure is now clearly attributable to the RBF policy lengthscale $\ell_s$, not
the cost function.

\paragraph{E-Strat5: $\ell_s = [0.15, 0.15]$ (10/10, mean 26\,mm).}
Setting $\ell_s = 0.15$\,m — matched to the target-zone width — achieves 10/10 hits
with errors in the range 20–33\,mm and a mean of 26\,mm.
This is the tightest absolute error band observed in the entire study, consistent with
the policy now having sufficient spatial resolution to discriminate targets within the
narrow zone.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Lengthscale-to-Range Scaling Rule}
\label{sec:study1:scaling}

The Config E diagnosis motivates a principled rule for choosing $\ell_s$.
Define the \emph{RBF sensitivity} between two target locations separated by distance
$d$ as

\begin{equation}
  S(d,\, \ell_s) = \exp\!\left( -\frac{d^2}{2\,\ell_s^2} \right).
  \label{eq:rbf_sensitivity}
\end{equation}

$S \to 1$ means the two RBF values are indistinguishable, so the policy outputs
identical speeds; $S \to 0$ means the two targets are well separated and the policy
can assign different speeds independently.

\paragraph{Calibration against Config A.}
Config A has a target range of $\approx 1.0$\,m and uses $\ell_s = 1.0$\,m.
Two targets at opposite ends of that range give
$S(1.0,\, 1.0) = e^{-0.5} \approx 0.607$ — distinguishable, and Config A converges.
Config E with the same $\ell_s$ gives $S(0.35,\, 1.0) = e^{-0.061} \approx 0.941$ —
essentially identical, explaining the constant-output failure.

\paragraph{The rule.}
Setting $\ell_s \approx 0.15 \times \Delta_{\mathrm{range}}$ yields $S \approx 0.066$,
the same level of discriminability as Config A:

\begin{equation}
  \ell_s^* \approx 0.15\;\Delta_{\mathrm{range}},
  \label{eq:ls_rule}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta_{\mathrm{range}} = \ell_M - \ell_{\min}$ is the width of the target
workspace.
Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity} verifies this across all configurations.

Equation~\eqref{eq:ls_rule} provides an \emph{a priori} design rule that practitioners
can apply before running any experiment: given the task geometry, choose $\ell_s$ so
that targets at opposite ends of the workspace achieve $S \lesssim 0.1$.
The rule is conservative — smaller $\ell_s$ increases the number of RBF centres
needed for coverage — but prevents the degenerate constant-output regime.
